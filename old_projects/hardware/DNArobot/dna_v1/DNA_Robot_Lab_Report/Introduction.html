Introduction to DNA:



Background Information:


Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA) is a nucleic acid (special molecule) that contains genetic instructions. These instructions can be considered blueprints for organisms, and are used in the development and functioning of all living organisms.


The DNA molecule is very long, and is composed of two main components: A sugar and phosphate backbone, and base pairs. The information that is stored in DNA is made up of four chemical bases: Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C) and Thymine (T). These bases bond with each other to form base-pairs (A bonds with T and G bonds with C). The base-pairs are connected to the sugar and phosphate backbone; on one end, the pair is bonded to a sugar and on the other end it is bonded to a phosphate. The combination of a base-pair attached to sugar and phosphate molecules is called a nucleotide. Nucleotides are arranged, one after another in long strands forming a double-helix spiral shape. The structure of a DNA molecule is similar to that of a ladder (with base-pairs as rungs). The structure, or order of these bases within the DNA molecule is what encodes the information; the arrangement determines all of the properties and traits of a particular organism. Specific sections of DNA, called genes, are what carry the information on the traits and properties of an organism. DNA is found within the cells of organisms; it is arranged in structures called chromosomes. A genome is all of the traits and properties of an organism that is stored as information within DNA. The human genome is made up of 23 chromosomes, and altogether contains 23,000 genes and about 3 billion DNA base-pairs.


The human genome is being digitized in the Human Genome Project, accessible through the Guttenberg Project. When digitizing DNA code, a DNA sequencer is used to determine and display in textual form, the sequence that DNA base pairs are bonded in.
